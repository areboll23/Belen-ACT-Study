English · Priority #1

Comma Rules

The ACT tests 6 specific comma rules over and over. Memorize these and you'll recognize the error instantly on test day.

Rule 1
Comma after an introductory phrase
When a sentence starts with a phrase before the main subject, put a comma after that phrase.
✗ After the storm the roads were flooded.
✓ After the storm, the roads were flooded.
Rule 2
Comma + conjunction to join two complete sentences
Use a comma before for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so (FANBOYS) when joining two independent clauses.
✗ She studied hard but she still felt nervous.
✓ She studied hard, but she still felt nervous.
Rule 3
NO comma splice — two sentences can't be joined by just a comma
A comma alone can NOT connect two complete sentences. Use a period, semicolon, or comma + conjunction.
✗ I finished the test, I felt relieved. ← WRONG
✓ I finished the test. I felt relieved.
✓ I finished the test; I felt relieved.
✓ I finished the test, and I felt relieved.
Rule 4
Commas around extra information (nonessential clauses)
If you can remove the phrase without changing the sentence's main meaning, put commas around it. Test: take it out — does the sentence still make sense?
✗ My sister who loves music played the piano.
✓ My sister, who loves music, played the piano.
Rule 5
Commas in a list of 3 or more items
Separate list items with commas. The comma before the last item (Oxford comma) is correct on the ACT.
✓ She bought apples, oranges, and bananas.
Rule 6
NO comma between a subject and its verb
Never put a comma that separates the subject from what it's doing.
✗ The dog, barked loudly. ← WRONG
✓ The dog barked loudly.

English · Priority #1

Sentence Structure

Sentence structure is the #1 most tested area in ACT English (20.5% of all questions). Learn to identify these errors and you're already ahead.

Concept 1
What makes a complete sentence?
A complete sentence needs: (1) a subject, (2) a verb, and (3) a complete thought. Missing any one of these = a fragment.
✗ Running through the park. ← No subject — fragment!
✓ She was running through the park.
Concept 2
Run-on sentences
Two complete sentences crammed together without proper punctuation. Fix with: (a) period, (b) semicolon, (c) comma + FANBOYS conjunction.
✗ The test was hard I studied for weeks. ← Run-on
✓ The test was hard. I studied for weeks.
✓ The test was hard; I studied for weeks.
✓ The test was hard, but I studied for weeks.
Concept 3
Semicolons — the period in disguise
A semicolon connects two complete sentences that are closely related. The rule: what's before AND after the semicolon must each be a complete sentence.
✗ She loves math; studying every night.
✓ She loves math; she studies every night.
Concept 4
Parallel structure — lists must match
When listing things, every item must be in the same grammatical form. If one item is a verb ending in -ing, they all should be.
✗ She likes running, to swim, and dance.
✓ She likes running, swimming, and dancing.
Concept 5
Colons — introducing a list or explanation
Use a colon after a complete sentence to introduce a list or explanation. What comes before the colon must be a complete sentence.
✗ My hobbies include: reading, hiking, and cooking.
✓ I have three hobbies: reading, hiking, and cooking.

English · Priority #1

Verb Tenses & Agreement

Two verb rules dominate the ACT: tense consistency and subject-verb agreement. Learn to spot both.

Rule 1
Tense consistency — don't switch tenses
If a passage is written in the past tense, keep all verbs in the past tense. Switching from past to present in the middle of a sentence is almost always wrong on the ACT.
✗ She walked into the room and sits down.
✓ She walked into the room and sat down.
Rule 2
Subject-verb agreement — singular with singular
A singular subject takes a singular verb. A plural subject takes a plural verb. The ACT hides the subject behind long phrases to trick you — always identify the REAL subject.
✗ The box of chocolates were left on the table.
✓ The box of chocolates was left on the table.
(Subject is "box" — singular, not "chocolates")
Rule 3
Tricky indefinite pronouns
These words are ALWAYS singular: everyone, someone, anyone, nobody, each, either, neither, one. They take singular verbs even though they feel plural.
✗ Everyone have finished their test.
✓ Everyone has finished their test.
Strategy
How to spot the real subject
Cross out everything between the subject and the verb. Whatever's left is what must agree.
The results of the experiment [that was conducted over many years] were surprising.
Cross out the bracket: "The results...were" ✓
English · Priority #1

Pronouns

Pronoun questions test two things: does the pronoun match its antecedent in number, and is it the right form (I vs. me, who vs. whom)?

Rule 1
Pronoun-antecedent agreement
A pronoun must match the noun it replaces in number (singular/plural). If the noun is singular, the pronoun must be singular too.
✗ Each student must bring their own pencil.
✓ Each student must bring his or her own pencil.
("Each" is singular → singular pronoun)
Rule 2
Its vs. It's — the most common trap
Its = possession (belonging to it). It's = it is. Test: can you replace it with "it is"? If yes → it's. If no → its.
✗ The dog wagged it's tail.
✓ The dog wagged its tail.
("Its" shows the tail belongs to the dog)
Rule 3
Who vs. Whom
Who = subject (doing the action). Whom = object (receiving the action). Trick: replace with he/she (→ who) or him/her (→ whom).
✓ Who called? (He called → who)
✓ To whom did you speak? (I spoke to him → whom)
Rule 4
Ambiguous pronouns
A pronoun must have a clear, single antecedent. If "it" or "they" could refer to more than one thing, the sentence is ambiguous — that's an ACT error.
✗ When the book hit the shelf, it broke.
(Did the book break or the shelf?)
✓ When the book hit the shelf, the shelf broke.
English · Priority #2

Transition Words

Transition questions ask you to pick the word that best connects two ideas. The key is understanding what relationship exists between the sentences.

Contrast
however · although · but · yet · nevertheless · on the other hand · despite · while · even though
Use when: sentence 2 says something different or unexpected from sentence 1.
The weather was cold. However, we still went outside.
Addition
furthermore · in addition · moreover · also · additionally · as well
Use when: sentence 2 adds more of the same idea.
She studied every night. Furthermore, she hired a tutor.
Cause / Effect
therefore · consequently · as a result · thus · so · because · since
Use when: sentence 2 is the result or consequence of sentence 1.
She studied hard. As a result, she scored well.
Time / Sequence
first · then · next · finally · subsequently · previously · meanwhile · ultimately · afterward
Use when: describing a sequence of events in order.
First, she read the passage. Then, she answered the questions.
Example / Emphasis
for example · for instance · specifically · indeed · in fact · notably · in particular
Use when: sentence 2 gives a specific example of sentence 1's claim.
Many animals migrate. For instance, monarch butterflies travel thousands of miles.
Strategy: Before picking a transition word, ask yourself — are these two sentences agreeing, disagreeing, showing cause/effect, or in sequence? That narrows it to one category.
English · Priority #2

Wordiness & Clarity

The ACT loves to test whether you can cut out unnecessary words. The shorter, cleaner option is almost always correct — as long as it preserves the meaning.

Rule 1
Shorter = usually better on the ACT
If two answers say the same thing but one is shorter, the shorter one is almost always correct. Redundancy (saying the same thing twice) is always wrong.
✗ She returned back to her home house where she lived.
✓ She returned home.
Rule 2
Vague filler words to delete
Words like really, very, quite, basically, somewhat, kind of, sort of, actually are usually fluff. The ACT will test your ability to cut them.
✗ The results were very quite significant.
✓ The results were significant.
Rule 3
Redundant pairs
Never use two words that mean the same thing together. These are always wrong on the ACT.
✗ end result · final outcome · past history · future plans · advance forward · new innovation · basic fundamentals
✓ result · outcome · history · plans · advance · innovation · fundamentals
Rule 4
DELETE is a valid answer
When the ACT offers "DELETE the underlined portion" as a choice, it's often the correct one. If removing the underlined text leaves a perfectly good sentence, delete it.
If deleting it doesn't change the meaning or break the grammar → delete it.
Science · Priority #2

Reading Data — Graphs, Tables & Charts

40–50% of ACT Science is just reading data correctly. These questions don't require science knowledge — they require careful reading.

Step 1
Always read the axes first
Before looking at the data, identify: What does the x-axis represent? What does the y-axis represent? What are the units? This takes 10 seconds and prevents misreading the whole graph.
Step 2
Identify the trend
Ask: as x increases, does y increase, decrease, or stay the same? Is the relationship linear (straight line) or curved? Does it level off at some point?
📈 As temperature increases, reaction rate increases → positive trend
📉 As altitude increases, air pressure decreases → negative trend
Step 3
Reading tables
Find the row and column the question asks about. Then read the value at their intersection. Don't guess based on memory — always look at the table.
Step 4
Comparing multiple lines/sets
When a graph has multiple lines (e.g., Trial 1, Trial 2, Trial 3), the question often asks you to compare them. Look at the legend first, then trace each line separately.
Strategy
The answer is always in the passage
ACT Science is an open-book test in a way — the answer to every data question is somewhere in the graphs, tables, or text. Never answer from outside knowledge alone. Find the evidence.
Science · Priority #2

Experiment Design — Scientific Investigation

These questions ask you to understand HOW an experiment was set up and WHY. Learn these 8 terms cold.

Hypothesis
A testable prediction made before the experiment. Usually "If [condition], then [outcome] because [reason]."
Independent Variable
The thing the scientist intentionally changes in an experiment. There's usually only one. It goes on the x-axis.
Dependent Variable
The thing the scientist measures as a result. It changes because of the independent variable. It goes on the y-axis.
Control Group
The group where nothing is changed — the baseline for comparison. All other groups are compared to the control.
Constant
Everything in the experiment that is kept the same across all groups, so you know any differences are caused by the independent variable.
Sample Size
How many subjects/trials. Larger sample size = more reliable results. ACT often asks: "How would increasing sample size affect the results?"
Valid Experiment
An experiment that: (1) only changes one variable at a time, (2) has a control group, (3) uses a large enough sample, and (4) controls for all confounding variables.
Conclusion
What the data shows. A conclusion must be supported by the data in the experiment — not by outside knowledge or assumptions.
Quick tip: When asked "what would make this experiment better?" the answer is almost always: add a control group, increase sample size, or control for a variable that wasn't held constant.
Science · Priority #1

Evaluating Models & Claims

This is your lowest-scoring area (10%). These questions ask: does this evidence support, weaken, or neither support nor weaken this claim?

The key question
What is the relationship between the evidence and the claim?
Every evaluation question boils down to: Does this new information make the claim more believable, less believable, or does it not affect the claim at all?
Type 1
Supports the claim
New evidence SUPPORTS a claim when it is consistent with what the claim predicts. Ask: "If the claim is true, would we expect to see this evidence?" If yes → supports.
Claim: "More sunlight causes faster plant growth."
Evidence: Plants given 12 hours of light grew 20% taller than those given 6 hours.
→ This SUPPORTS the claim ✓
Type 2
Weakens (refutes) the claim
Evidence WEAKENS a claim when it contradicts what the claim predicts. Ask: "If the claim is true, would this evidence be unexpected?" If yes → weakens.
Claim: "More sunlight causes faster plant growth."
Evidence: Plants in full sun for 16 hours showed stunted growth.
→ This WEAKENS the claim ✗
Type 3
Neither supports nor weakens
Evidence is irrelevant if it measures something completely unrelated to what the claim is about. Don't force a connection that isn't there.
Strategy
The 3-step approach
1. Identify the claim (what is being argued)
2. Identify the new evidence
3. Ask: does the evidence match or contradict the claim?

Never use outside knowledge — only what's in the passage.
Math · Priority #3

Functions — f(x)

You got 20% on functions. After this lesson, you'll understand exactly what f(x) means and how to use it.

What it means
f(x) = a machine that takes a number and spits out another
f(x) is just a rule. You put a number in (x), the function does something to it, and gives you a result. f(x) means "f of x" — the output of function f when the input is x.
f(x) = 2x + 3
f(4) = 2(4) + 3 = 8 + 3 = 11
Just replace every x with 4 and calculate.
How to evaluate
Plug and chug — always
Whatever is inside the parentheses replaces every x in the formula. That's all there is to it.
g(x) = x² − 5
g(3) = (3)² − 5 = 9 − 5 = 4
g(−2) = (−2)² − 5 = 4 − 5 = −1
Composite functions
f(g(x)) — functions inside functions
Work from the inside out. Solve g(x) first, then plug that result into f.
f(x) = x + 1 and g(x) = 2x
f(g(3)) = f(2×3) = f(6) = 6 + 1 = 7
Reading graphs
f(x) on a graph — inputs and outputs
On a graph, the x-axis is the INPUT and the y-axis is the OUTPUT. "What is f(3)?" → find x = 3 on the x-axis, go straight up to the line, read the y-value.
If the graph passes through (3, 7) → f(3) = 7
Domain & Range
What x values are allowed?
Domain = all allowed inputs (x values). Range = all possible outputs (y values). Watch for values that would cause division by zero or a square root of a negative number — those are excluded from the domain.
f(x) = 1/(x−2): x cannot equal 2 (would divide by zero)
Domain = all real numbers except x = 2
Math · Priority #4

Statistics & Probability

You got 33% here. These are very learnable — a handful of formulas cover 90% of what the ACT tests.

Mean
The average — most common ACT stats concept
Add all values, divide by how many there are.
Data: 10, 15, 20, 25
Mean = (10+15+20+25) ÷ 4 = 70 ÷ 4 = 17.5
Median
The middle value
Put the numbers in order. If odd count → middle number. If even count → average the two middle numbers.
Odd: 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 → Median = 7
Even: 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13 → Median = (7+9)÷2 = 8
Mode
The most frequent value
The number that appears most often. A data set can have multiple modes or no mode.
2, 3, 3, 5, 7, 3, 9 → Mode = 3 (appears 3 times)
Range
Spread of the data
Range = largest value − smallest value
2, 5, 8, 12, 19 → Range = 19 − 2 = 17
Probability
The formula you must know
P(event) = (favorable outcomes) ÷ (total possible outcomes). Always a number between 0 and 1 (or 0% to 100%).
Bag: 4 red, 6 blue, 5 green = 15 total
P(blue) = 6/15 = 2/5
P(NOT blue) = 9/15 = 3/5
Two-event probability
AND means multiply
For two independent events both happening, multiply their individual probabilities.
P(heads) = 1/2. Two coin flips:
P(heads AND heads) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4
